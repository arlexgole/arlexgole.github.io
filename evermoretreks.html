October 2018 - Ongoing

Solo Side Project

I have always wanted to help my home country of Nepal, but I felt under qualified and was never really was sure how to go about it. Recently, I became familiar with the concept of “social entrepreneurship” and have grown to truly admire social enterprises that have been able to successfully scale their organization while making a difference in the world (Warby Parker, MealShare, TOMS, and many others). Inspired and motivated, I want to use my design and business skills to create my own social enterprise in Nepal.

High-Level Problems

1. Despite making improvements in recent years, Nepal remains one of the poorest and least developed countries in the world.

2. Tourism is one of the largest industries in Nepal and plays a key role in the economy. However, it is facing many challenges, such as environmental, technological, and external competition.

Research (Interviews)

I interviewed people with years of experience in Nepal’s tourism industry including tour guides, local business owners, and a manager of a trekking agency. Setting up these interviews was a bit challenging since I was on the other side of the world. Nonetheless, I was able to gain a holistic perspective of the industry.

Key Insights:

  • Trekking agencies get clients through many different channels including their physical office, referrals from hotels, and online bookings. Each channel has its advantages and disadvantages.
  • Trekking prices vary depending on many different factors (group size, lodging, quality of guides, ability to bargain, vulnerability of tourists, etc.).
  • Due to the profitability of the tourism industry, it is an extremely competitive industry. Unhealthy competition has led to price wars, dodgy operators, and many unethical business practices.

Competitive Analysis

Many of the bigger trekking agencies in Nepal have a website that provides information about their company and trek offerings. I analyzed these websites to learn more about the current competition and identify any strengths or weaknesses that I can build on.

Key Insights:

  • Most agencies don’t have a big focus on sustainability.
  • Detail pages are text heavy.
  • Limited ways to filter through treks (usually just location).
  • Some agencies don’t offer online payments or even show the prices.

Opportunities:

  • Focusing on sustainable tourism by educating visitors and investing part of the profits back into the local communities.
  • Targeting beginner trekkers by offering relatively easy treks and educating them about trekking.
  • Providing a comprehensive and seamless online experience.

Personas

Based on the research I conducted, I created two goal-directed personas to better understand the users' behaviors, beliefs, and goals. These personas helped guide my ideation phase by serving as a reminder of the users I was designing for.

Journey Map

Using these personas, I created a journey map to visualize the user's entire experience. Creating this map helped me better identify key problems and areas of opportunity.

Wireframes

After having a solid understanding of the the tourism industry and identifying the key problems for my target users, I began wireframing screens. One of my main goals while wireframing was to explore as many different variations as possible.

User Testing

Before diving into higher fidelity designs, I wanted to get feedback from users. I focused my user testing sessions on the landing page and the detail page because I believe those are the two most important pages of the website.

Key Insights - Landing Page:

  • Users did not understand what trekking meant. How was it different from tours? Is it like hiking? Is it like camping?
  • Users liked the tagline but didn’t really understand what it meant. Users wanted more information about how they were helping local communities.
  • For design A and C, users stated that all the pictures look very similar and that there isn’t much information for the user to decide which trek to pick.
  • 4/5 users like design B the best because it provides additional helpful information.

Key Insights - Detail Page:

  • Users did not understand what moderate difficulty meant.
  • For design A, most users liked having the option to call / send a message.
  • For design B, most users stated that the information was easier to digest.
  • 3/5 users perferred design B.

Iterations

I iterated on my designs to incorporate the insights learned from the user testing sessions. I focused on the landing page and the detail page first because they are the most important screens. I also had to exclude certain features such as user reviews and filter options in order to prioritize the most important features for first MVP.

Landing Page:

  • Using a video (~ 1min 30 sec) to better educate users about trekking and the company mission.
  • Using the design B format to highlight certain treks.

Detail Page:

  • Combining designs A and B. The left side makes the content digestible and easy to read. The right side makes it easy to start the booking process and provides constant access to navigation and contact information.
  • Showing the highlights of the trek first to show the most exciting aspects right away.
  • Including a FAQ section to provide more information about the trek.

Takeways

It has been a very challenging but rewarding experience to work at the intersection of entrepreneurship and design. Although my high-level problem scope still seems very daunting, I believe that using the process of human centered design, I have taken a step in the right direction.

I plan to continue working on this project and my goal is launch the website by the end of the year. In order to accomplish this goal, it will require many more cycles of research, design, and user testing.
